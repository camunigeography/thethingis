How to Use a Sun Compass

A Sun Compass is complementary to a sundial. A sundial tells the time by the direction in which a shadow is cast, but it first has to be aligned correctly: the edge that casts the shadow (the gnomon) must point south if the dial is to be used in the northern hemisphere, and north in the southern hemisphere. The idea of the sun compass is that, if you know the time and the direction of the gnomon’s shadow, you can deduce the alignment of the compass. In fact, it is just a rotatable sundial: you simply rotate the dial (which has to be held horizontal) until the shadow shows the correct time, and then the gnomon must be pointing north or south depending on the hemisphere.

A sun compass or a sundial has to be designed for a particular latitude, although in practice it can be used within a few degrees of that latitude without too much error. In the northern hemisphere the time marks increase clockwise around the dial, whereas in the southern hemisphere they increase anticlockwise (this kind of sun dial or compass does not work at the equator!) The spacing of the time marks becomes more uniform the closer the design latitude is to the north or south pole. Wilson’s sun compass has clearly been designed to be used at a high southerly latitude.
