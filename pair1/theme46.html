Geology and Antarctica

A geologist studies the Earth to understand how it was made, how it has changed and how it is changing now. To do this, a geologist looks at the rocks to see what type they are and if they have been folded or faulted by major earth movements. The geological history of an area can then be deduced and compared with the geology of adjacent areas. If there are fossils present in the rocks they can indicate the age of the rocks and may also show how these fossil animals or plants have evolved over time. Today there are many different types of specialist geologists and when their different research results are combined they lead to a much deeper understanding of our planet.

Antarctica is one of seven continents that comprise the land area of our planet. It may be the most remote of the continents, and the only one that has never had an indigenous human population, but it is still an integral part of the planet as a whole. If we are to understand the Earth as a whole then we must study the geology of Antarctica. If we do not do this it would be like trying to complete a jigsaw puzzle with many pieces missing and no picture to show how the finished jigsaw should appear.

Peter Clarkson, 2014
