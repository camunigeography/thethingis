Constantin Brancusi 1876–1957

Brancusi was born in Hobitza, Romania. In 1904 he moved to Paris, where he spent the rest of his life. His early work was influenced by traditional Romanian carving, but in Paris, under Rodin’s influence, he gradually evolved a pure, abstract manner that strove to depict the formal essence of objects without rejecting the natural world.

Most of his subsequent marble carvings and bronzes consisted of variations on a limited number of themes (heads, birds, fish, etc.) simplified almost to the point of abstraction, with smooth surfaces and an emphasis on pure basic forms such as the ovoid; his wood carvings were usually closer to African art and to the Romanian folk tradition of wood carving. Five of his works exhibited at the Armory Show, New York, in 1913 attracted great attention and led to his first one-man exhibition at the Photo-Secession Gallery, New York, in 1914, followed by three further US exhibitions, most of his sculptures entering American collections.

Brancusi died in Paris in 1957 and he bequeathed his studio and its contents to the Musée National d’Art Moderne, Paris.
