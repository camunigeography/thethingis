Unicornu Fossile-Canstadiense: Fragments of a Mammoth Tusk
Pieces of fossil mammoth tusk were sent to Dr Woodward by two of his correspondents Johan Jakob Scheuchzer (1672–1733) and ‘M. Valkenier’ (probably Pieter Valkenier (1641–1712)) and described as “Unicornu Fossile-Canstadiense” (fossil unicorn of Konstanz).

The fragments come from Bad Cannstatt near Stuttgart in Germany. These and the accompanying mammoth tooth may all have come from a famous excavation that took place there in 1701.

Fossil mammoth tusks were often identified as unicornu verum (true unicorn) as they were believed by some to be the horns of unicorns, a type of mythical beast. Unicornu verum was an expensive medicine that was used to counter poisoning and venomous bites and to cure wounds. Woodward’s more down to earth identification is ‘Pieces of large tooth’.

Johan Jakob Scheuchzer was a physician based in Zurich, and was famous for his ‘Homo diluvii testis’ a fossil giant salamander he thought was the skeleton of a human killed during the biblical flood.

This mammoth tooth accompanied the Unicornu Fossile-Canstadiense fragments. The tooth is a molar from the right lower jaw of a mammoth, a type of extinct elephant. It was found at Bad Cannstatt near Stuttgart in Germany. Woodward describes the specimen as ‘A very large Grinder of some cetaceous Fish’ by which he means the tooth of a whale.

Woodward’s collection and his published catalogue demonstrates that he was highly skilled at correctly identifying fossils. However, his failure to correctly identify this tooth as belonging to a type of elephant suggests that he had not been able to make detailed anatomical studies of the bones and teeth of living elephants and whales.

Dan Pemberton, Collections Manager, Sedgwick Museum of Earth Sciences.
