Conserving Watercolours

Watercolours like these are very vulnerable to irreversible fading and colour changes, which can alter the relationships between colours in a picture. This is caused by exposure to light and ultraviolet (UV) rays, so we try to protect them as much as possible by keeping them in the dark and only displaying them for short periods. When a watercolour is used in an exhibition, we put it at the darker end of the gallery and reduce the amount of light that falls directly onto it ... ideally without making it so dark that nobody can appreciate the fine details! The conservators here also monitor the amount of light and UV in the gallery using telemetric dataloggers – you may spot one as you go round this exhibition.

Preserving the original colours is particularly important because these watercolours are more than just beautiful artworks. In an age when all photography and film was in black and white, expedition artists were able to document details of the polar landscape that could not be captured on film. These two watercolours use a range of blue and grey pigments to show some of the subtle colours and light effects found in the South Indian Ocean. Wilson’s other watercolours document the wildlife found in Antarctica, as well as other points of scientific interest.

Christina Rozeik, SPRI Conservator
