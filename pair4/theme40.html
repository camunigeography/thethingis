Making a Herbarium Sheet

Christine Bartram
Chief Technician, Herbarium
University of Cambridge

Botanical specimens are carefully arranged and pressed in a plant press to extract moisture while preserving the morphological integrity of the plant. The dried material is then mounted. Mounting is the process of attaching a dried pressed plant and its label to a sheet of heavy paper. This provides physical support that allows the specimen to be handled and stored long-term with a minimum of damage.

Prior to attachment, the specimen and its label are arranged on the paper to allow maximum observation of diagnostic (usually reproductive) features as well as the range of variation in vegetative structures, including both sides of the leaves. Plants are generally positioned in a life-like arrangement with lower stem toward the bottom of the sheet and flowers toward the top. A small packet may be fixed to the sheet to contain fragments of the specimen that break off over time. Once the best arrangement of the specimen has been determined, it is attached to the paper using strips of gummed linen cloth tape. Large or bulky items may need to be sewn onto the sheet with linen thread. The objective is to secure the specimen firmly to the mounting paper, while leaving the plant loose enough to be removed for study if necessary. Because herbarium specimens are intended for long-term study and storage, it is critical that that all supplies used for mounting are durable.
