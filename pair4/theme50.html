Antarctic Fossils and Plate Tectonics

Things on planet earth don’t stay in the same place: continents break up and move. 160 million years ago, Antarctica was part of the supercontinent Gondwanaland, which also contained modern-day Africa, the Arabian peninsula, Australia, the Indian subcontinent, Madagascar and South America. This landmass split apart and its various pieces spread in different directions around the planet.

Today, the movements of landmasses are explained by the theory of plate tectonics. The earth’s surface is made up of separate ‘plates’ which move across the planet’s molten core, carrying continents. Their collisions cause volcanic activity and when they pull apart they form rift valleys and mid-oceanic ridges. The polar researcher Alfred Lothar Wegener laid the foundation of this theory when he came up with the idea of continental drift in 1915. He argued that the continents moved around the surface of the planet, but he couldn’t explain how – that came fifty years later.

One of his chief pieces of evidence was the discovery of closely related fossils on opposite sides of the world. Specimens found in Antarctica in the early part of the twentieth century matched examples from Africa, South America, Australia and India; this proved that the same species had once lived in all of these locations. The best explanation for such a discovery was that these places had once been connected up and later moved apart.

The members of the British Antarctic (Terra Nova) Expedition, 1910–13, did not know that the fossils they collected would contribute to such a major step forward in our understanding of the planet. At the time, the discovery of fossilized leaves and trees was enough to make them certain that Antarctica’s climate had once been much warmer. In 1914, examining the fossil specimens brought back from the expedition, geologist Alfred Charles Seward wrote that the evidence ‘renders it difficult to escape from the conclusion that the ancient continent of Gondwana extended to within a short distance of the South Pole or even to the Pole itself, whether as a continuous continent or as an archipelago of islands cannot be determined.’ In fact, it turned out to be the other way round: Gondwanaland didn’t extend all the way to the pole, Antarctica drifted south to get there!
