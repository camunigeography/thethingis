Fossil collection and the Terra Nova Expedition

The collection of geological specimens and fossils formed a key part of the research work of the Antarctic expeditions of the heroic age of polar exploration (1901–22). Dr Edward Wilson, Chief of Scientific Staff on Captain Scott’s British Antarctic (Terra Nova) Expedition, 1910–13, was so committed to the importance of such specimens that he refused to offload from the sledge those he had collected on the journey to the South Pole, even when malnutrition and exhaustion were driving the men towards death. The specimens were retrieved when the bodies of Wilson, Scott and Henry ‘Birdie’ Bowers were found.

During World War II, the Trustees of the British Museum (Natural History) (now the Natural History Museum) in London sent a number of the fossils picked up during the Terra Nova expedition to the Sedgwick Museum in Cambridge. The following is a transcript of the correspondence with Walter Campbell-Smith, an authority on Antarctic fossils:

Letter from Walter Campbell-Smith (to the Professor of Geology, University of Cambridge) relating to the Terra Nova fossil specimens

29 September 1944

I am instructed by the Trustees to send you a small selection of duplicates from the collections of the British Antarctic Expedition 1910–1913. These specimens now being despatched are from the Slate-Greywacke Formation of Robertson Bay, described in British Antarctic (Terra Nova) Expedition, 1910, Natural History Report, Geology, Vol 1. No. 4, No 4b, dated 1921.

You have already received representative specimens of the plutonic and metamorphic rocks collected by this expedition. A selection of the Volcanic rocks are being sent under separate cover and list is enclosed.

It is possible that the specimens of plutonic and metamorphic rocks have been handed over to the Department of Mineralogy and Petrology. You will be quite free to hand over these specimens now sent, to the same Department if you wish to do so.

Yours faithfully.
W.Campbell-Smith

Copy of Reply [presumably from Cecil Edgar Tilley?]

Dear Sir
The Professor of Geology has handed over to me with your letter a selection of duplicates from the collections of the B.A.E, 1910–1913 which you forwarded to him on September 29th.

The Department of Mineralogy and Petrology is most grateful to receive this collection which will be added to the Polar Collections which contain representative specimens of plutonic and metamorphic rocks collected by the British Antarctic Expedition.

I am happy to note from your letter that Cambridge may receive later a set of volcanic rocks.

Yours faithfully
