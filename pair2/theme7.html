Apsley Cherry Garrard's Diary

'Ulysses' in Apsley Cherry–Garrard's Diary

These images show the pages of Apsley Cherry-Garrard’s diary in which he quotes from Tennyson’s ‘Ulysses’.

The first quotation comes at the end of a section of the diary entitled ‘Written on the barrier after finding the remains of the southern party’, after a gruelling account of the discovery of the frozen bodies of Captain Scott, Edward Wilson and Henry ‘Birdie’ Bowers. Cherry-Garrard quotes consoling, fortifying material from a number of sources. On this particular page, he quotes the Epilogue to Robert Browning’s last collection Asolando, followed by the final line of ‘Ulysses’:


At the midnight in the silence of the sleep-time,
When you set your fancies free,
Will they pass to where―by death, fools think, imprisoned―
Low he lies who once so loved you, whom you loved so,
―Pity me?

Oh to love so, be so loved, yet so mistaken!
What had I on earth to do
With the slothful, with the mawkish, the unmanly?
Like the aimless, helpless, hopeless, did I drivel!
―Being―who?

One who never turned his back but marched breast forward,
Never doubted clouds would break,
Never dreamed, though right were worsted, wrong would triumph,
Held we fall to rise, are baffled to fight better,
Sleep to wake.

No, at noonday in the bustle of man’s work-time
Greet the unseen with a cheer!
Bid him forward, breast and back as either should be,
‘Strive and thrive!’ cry, ‘Speed,―fight on, fare ever
There as here!’

R. Browning. Epilogue


"One equal temper of heroic hearts…
To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield."


The second comes in the entry for Monday 20 January, 1913, recounting the erection of the memorial on Observation Hill:

We left the ship at 8 a.m. with the Cross for Observation Hill – Atkinson, Wright, Debenham and self, with Crean, Abbott, Davies and Keohane. We were landed some one and a half miles south of Tent Island, and I should think four miles west of Glacier Tongue. This sea-ice seems pretty good here, but it is heavy pulling on soft snow, and there has been no wind out as far as this from the land for a very long time. The Cross is a very fine one, and I am glad that my suggestion for a motto has been taken: "To strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield." Tennyson. "Ulysses."

Notably, Cherry-Garrard misattributes the quotation to Robert Browning, the other pre-eminent poet of the day. It has been subsequently corrected to ‘Tennyson Ulysses’ in conspicuous red pencil!
