The Memorial Cross on Observation Hill

These images depict the cross erected in memory of Captain Scott, Dr Edward Wilson, Lt Henry ‘Birdie’ Bowers, Captain Lawrence Oates and Petty Officer Edgar Evans on Observation Hill, at the southern tip of Ross Island in Antarctica, as the expedition ships left Antarctica for the last time in January 1913.

In his journal entry for 23 January, 1913, Frank Debenham, the geologist who founded the Scott Polar Research Institute in 1920, describes erecting the cross:

The next morning we took the cross up the hill in pieces, rather heavy work and by 4 in the afternoon, Chippy had got it up and painted and we were ready to return.

It is an immense cross of West Australian Jarrah, and is 12 ft long by 6 inches through. It is on the top of Observation Hill which is a solitary hill of unusually pretty shape at the extreme South of Ross Island overlooking the barrier. To the West one sees the Western Mts and Mts Lister and Discovery, while to the south lies White Island and then the blank waste of the Barrier, a dead white plain hardly differing from the sky in colour.

It is altogether a most fitting place for the memorial cross and the cross itself is worthy of it. It will last as long as the rocks in which it is placed.

Debenham then sketches the inscription itself, including the quotation from ‘Ulysses’ at the bottom, and records having taken twelve photos. The photo of the cross here may be one of them, although the precise attribution has been lost.
