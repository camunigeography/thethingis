The Odyssey

The Greek poet Homer’s epic poem The Odyssey tells the story of the hero Odysseus’ ten year struggle to return home after the Trojan war. He blinds the Cyclops Polyphemus, witnesses his crew be transformed into pigs, listens to the fatal song of the Sirens, visits the land of the dead, escapes the sea monsters Scylla and Charybdis and spends seven years imprisoned by the nymph Calypso, who is in love with him. When he finally returns home, he finds his house besieged by suitors who think he is dead and want to marry his wife, Penelope. With the help of his son, Telemachus, he kills them all. The cast of Odysseus’ head exhibited here comes from a sculpture depicting the passage in which Odysseus and his men kill Polyphemus.

The Odyssey, along with its precursor, Homer’s Iliad, is one of the oldest works of Western literature, thought to have been written towards the end of the 8th century BC in Ionia, an area of Ancient Greece which is now part of Turkey. It is often thought to have originated as an oral work, intended to be heard rather than read. Its opening lines introduce Odysseus:

Sing to me of the man, Muse, the man of twists and turns …
driven time and again off course, once he had plundered
the hallowed heights of Troy.
Many cities of men he saw and learned their minds,
many pains he suffered, heartsick on the open sea,
fighting to save his life and bring his comrades home.

The Odyssey, trans. Robert Fagles, Book I, ll. 1–6
